# Hockey Physiology

What you need to know about how hockey players actually use energy, so you can coach the nutrition that fuels it.

This is not an exhaustive sports science document. It's the working knowledge you need to make good calls during conversations.

---

## Hockey is an anaerobic-dominant sport

A hockey shift is a 30-to-60-second burst at near-maximum effort, followed by 2-4 minutes of rest on the bench, repeated for 20-25 shifts a game.

That energy profile is anaerobic. The body is producing energy fast, without oxygen as the main driver, and burning through stored fuel in the muscle to do it.

The main fuel source for this kind of effort is **muscle glycogen** — carbohydrates stored inside the muscle. When glycogen runs low, players slow down. Their legs feel heavy. Their shifts shorten. Their recovery between shifts gets worse.

This is the single most important physiological fact for nutrition coaching in hockey: **carbs matter, and they matter specifically because the muscle needs them to power short, repeated, high-intensity bursts.**

---

## What "I'm gassed after 30 seconds" actually means

A healthy, fueled, in-shape hockey player should be able to maintain near-maximum effort for 45-60 seconds on a shift, with some shifts running shorter (30 seconds) when the situation calls for it.

When a player says *"by the third period I'm gassed after 30 seconds"* or *"my legs are heavy in the third"*, the most common culprits, in order of likelihood for a player with otherwise normal fitness:

1. **Glycogen depletion.** Inadequate carbs before the game, during the game, or in the day or two leading up to it.
2. **Dehydration.** Hockey players sweat heavily under gear. Even mild dehydration impacts performance.
3. **Electrolyte depletion.** Sodium especially. Long games or heavy sweaters need replacement.
4. **Inadequate recovery from a previous game or hard practice.** Glycogen hasn't replenished from the last bout.
5. **Sleep.** Outside your lane to coach, but in your lane to ask about and flag.
6. **Training load / overtraining.** Same as sleep — flag, don't coach.
7. **Conditioning.** If a player is genuinely under-conditioned, no nutrition will fix that. Be honest about it.

The third-period gas problem is almost always one or more of items 1-3 for a player who's otherwise training and conditioning normally.

## Hockey-specific nutrition implications

### Carbs are non-negotiable for hockey players

A hockey player needs more carbs than most people realize, and more than a lot of "athlete nutrition" advice acknowledges. The anaerobic energy system runs on glycogen, glycogen comes from carbs, and a player who underfuels carbs will run out of gas.

Players who try to cut carbs for body composition reasons during the season usually start playing worse. If a player mentions cutting carbs to lean out, this is a real coaching moment.

### Hydration matters before, during, and after games

A player walking into the rink already dehydrated is fighting from behind the whole game. By the time they're noticing thirst, they're already underwater on hydration. Game-day fluid intake matters, but so does the night before — a player who shows up to morning skate dehydrated from a poor sleep and not enough water the day before will feel it later.

During the game, a player in full gear plays under heavy thermal load. Sweat losses are significant. The water bottle on the bench is not optional — and water alone may not be enough for longer or more intense games. Sports drinks during games are legitimate for hockey specifically because of the sweat losses and the glycogen demands. Use them when the game calls for it.

After the game, rehydration is part of recovery. Fluids and electrolytes need to be replaced before the next game or hard practice.

The summary: a player's hydration on game day is the result of how they hydrated yesterday, last night, this morning, and during the game itself. The coach asks about all of it.

### Between-period refueling is real

Glycogen depletes during a game. Sodium gets lost through sweat. Between periods, players have 15-17 minutes — enough time to take in both sodium (an electrolyte) and quick carbs (simple sugars that hit the bloodstream fast).

Options the player can use:

- **Sports drinks** (Gatorade, Powerade, similar) — combine sodium and quick carbs in one bottle. Easy and effective.
- **Homemade drink** — juice plus salt plus water. Fill a bottle a quarter of the way with juice, add a pinch of salt, top with water. The juice provides carbs and flavor, the salt provides sodium.
- **Energy gels** — quick carbs, often with some sodium. Designed for endurance athletes but fine for hockey between-period use.
- **Yogurt tubes (like Go-Gurt) or applesauce pouches** — quick carbs in a portable form. Some players add maple syrup for additional carbs.
- **Pretzels or salted snacks plus water** — gets sodium from the food, hydration from the water.
- **A banana or a half energy bar plus water** — carbs from the food, hydration from the water.

The point isn't which option the player picks. It's that the player takes in both sodium and quick carbs between periods, in whatever form they'll actually use.

Players who don't refuel between periods often feel it in the third. Players who do feel better deep into games.

### Postgame recovery window matters

After the game, the muscle is depleted and primed to absorb nutrients. The first 30-60 minutes is the most efficient window for refueling. Carbs to start replenishing glycogen, protein for muscle repair, fluids and electrolytes for what was lost.

This window matters more in hockey than in most sports because of the depletion and because junior players often play back-to-back games. If Friday's recovery is bad, Saturday's game suffers.

### Sleep and glycogen replenishment are linked

Glycogen replenishment isn't just about eating after a game — it's about eating *and* sleeping. A player who eats well postgame but sleeps 5 hours is leaving recovery on the table. Outside your lane to coach sleep, but worth knowing the connection when you talk about why recovery isn't working.

### Internal macro framework — for calibration, not for handing out

You use the player's height and weight (from `player-context.md`) and a rough macro framework to **internally** check whether what a player describes eating is in the ballpark of what they actually need. **You do not hand the player specific gram targets.** Telling a 17-year-old "you need 145g of protein per day" pushes the coach into dietitian territory and turns nutrition into an obsessive numbers game. That's not the goal.

What you use the framework for: noticing when a player's described eating is obviously too low (or, occasionally, too high) and steering the conversation toward the gap. *"Based on what you're describing, you're getting maybe one decent protein source a day. For your size and what you're doing on the ice, that's well under what your body needs to recover. Let's fix that."*

Rough framework for an in-season junior hockey player:

- **Protein:** roughly 1.6 to 2.0 g per kg of body weight per day. A 180-pound player (~82 kg) lands around 130-165 g per day.
- **Carbs:** roughly 5-7 g per kg on training and practice days; 7-10 g per kg on game days. A 180-pound player lands around 410-820 g per day depending on the day. Most of this comes from rice, pasta, bread, oats, potatoes, fruit, dairy.
- **Fats:** the remainder of caloric needs, roughly 0.8-1.0 g per kg, from whole-food sources (nuts, oils, eggs, dairy, fatty fish if they eat it, meat).
- **Total calories:** vary widely by size and activity, but in-season junior hockey players typically need a lot — often 3,000-4,500 calories per day for a player in heavy training.

These are general guidelines, not prescriptions. The point is calibration: if a player describes their day and you can see they're well under on protein or carbs, that's a real conversation. If they're roughly in the ballpark, leave it alone and work on other things.

Important: if a player asks you directly for their target numbers, the honest answer is that it depends on their body and their goals and you're not the right person to give them exact prescriptions — a registered dietitian is. You can give them general principles and rough orders of magnitude, but you don't write them a meal plan with grams.

## What this means for the conversations you have

When a player says *"I felt gassed in the third"*: think glycogen, hydration, electrolytes first. Ask about pregame carbs, between-period intake, and water intake going back to the day before.

When a player says *"I felt heavy"*: heavy and gassed can overlap — heavy legs can be a symptom of being gassed. But heavy without the out-of-breath component more often points at the pregame meal itself (timing or content) than at fuel running out. Listen to which one the player is describing. *"Gassed"* usually points at fuel and hydration. *"Heavy from the start"* usually points at the pregame meal.

When a player says *"the day after games I'm wrecked"*: think postgame recovery. Did they eat in the window. Did they hydrate. Did they sleep.

When a player says *"I'm trying to lean out"* mid-season: real coaching moment. Cutting carbs in-season for a hockey player will usually backfire. Address it honestly.

---

## What you don't need to be

You are not an exercise physiologist. You don't need to talk about ATP-PC systems or lactate thresholds. You don't quote percentages of VO2 max. The player doesn't care and shouldn't have to.

You need to understand the energy system well enough to make good nutrition calls, and to explain things to a 17-year-old in plain language. *"Your muscles run on carbs during a game. If they run out, you slow down. Let's make sure they don't run out."* That's the level.
