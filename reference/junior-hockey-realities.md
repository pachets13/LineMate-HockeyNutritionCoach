# Junior Hockey Realities

The lived realities of junior hockey life that a generic nutrition coach would miss. Read this so you can coach inside the actual world the player lives in, not a hypothetical world where they have full control of their food.

---

## The billet life

Most players in junior hockey live in billet houses — host families in the team's town who take in players for the season. Billets are not nutritionists. They are usually decent people doing a hard job, and the food they provide reflects their own habits more than any athletic plan.

Billet arrangements vary. Common patterns:

- **Billet cooks most dinners.** The player has dinner more or less handled, but it's whatever the billet family eats. Sometimes great. Sometimes pasta-and-chicken every night. Sometimes the kids' food (tacos, mac and cheese, pizza). The player usually controls breakfast, lunch, and snacks.
- **Billet cooks inconsistently.** Sometimes dinner is on, sometimes the player is on their own. Depends on the billet's schedule, their own kids, their mood. The player needs default plans for the nights they're on their own.
- **Billet handles groceries but not cooking.** The player has the kitchen, the ingredients, full responsibility for meals. This is actually a good setup if the player can cook.
- **Billet provides ingredients and a kitchen but nothing else.** Full player responsibility for cooking, but groceries handled.

**Single-player billet vs. multi-player billet.** Some billets host one player. Many host two or three players from the same team. The dynamic shifts when multiple players live in the same billet house:

- Players often eat together, which is a peer-pressure dynamic the coach should be aware of (if the older player eats junk, the younger one often follows).
- Meal patterns may be set around what works for the whole group, not the individual.
- Grocery requests may need to be coordinated.
- Players sometimes compare notes on what the billet cooks vs. what they wish they could eat.

Junior players almost always live with a billet family. Teams do not typically put players in their own apartments — players are too young, and the billet system is the established model for getting players housed, fed, and looked after. If a player describes "living alone" or "living in team housing alone," gently double-check — it's rare enough to be worth asking about, and the player might mean something different (rooming with another player without a host family, for example).

**One reliable pattern across most billets:** they handle groceries. If a player needs specific ingredients, the billet will usually pick them up. Asking for groceries is easier than asking the billet to cook differently.

**Social dynamics matter.** A 17-year-old isn't going to march into the billet kitchen and demand quinoa. They're a guest in someone's home. Coach the player on what's reasonable to ask for — a side of vegetables, a leaner protein swap, an extra plate for leftovers — versus what isn't (overhauling the family meal).

## Bus life

Junior hockey teams travel by bus. A lot. Routes can be:

- **Short hops** (under 2 hours): same-day there-and-back, game, home that night.
- **Half-day rides** (2-4 hours): same-day there-and-back, but the bus is part of the pregame.
- **Long rides** (4-6 hours): bus is the pregame window. Player eats on the bus.
- **Overnight / multi-game trips:** hotel involved, multiple games, food more variable.

**On a bus, the player's food is whatever they brought, whatever the team provides, or whatever the bus stops at.** Some teams have predictable stop patterns ("we always stop at the Subway in Steinbach on the way to Winkler"). Some teams provide pre-portioned meals. Some teams expect you to bring your own.

The container the player brings is the player's actual pregame meal. It needs to travel, hold up for hours, and feel like food the player wants to eat at hour three on a bus.

Common team-provided road food: pasta with chicken or meat sauce, subs, pizza (yes, really, sometimes), buffet-style chicken-and-rice from a hotel. Quality varies wildly.

## Team meal patterns

Most junior teams have predictable food patterns the player will learn over the season:

- **Pregame meals** on road trips often come from the same restaurants in each town. The player will figure out what's available in each city.
- **Postgame meals** are often team-provided on the road and player-responsibility at home.
- **Team dinners** for bonding or before big games happen occasionally. Often at restaurants, not always optimized for nutrition.

Capture these patterns in `schedule.md` as the player learns them. *"Brandon — pregame is always Boston Pizza, postgame is hotel restaurant."* Once that's written down, you don't have to re-ask.

## The hotel reality

On overnight road trips, the player is in a hotel for one or more nights. Hotels mean:

- **Breakfast is usually a hotel buffet or continental.** Eggs and toast or yogurt and fruit if you're lucky. Often pastries, cereal, juice. The player picks from what's there.
- **Lunch can be team-provided, room service, or a walk to a nearby restaurant.**
- **Pregame can be team-provided or eaten in-room.** A player who wants to bring their own pregame meal needs fridge access — most hotel rooms have a mini-fridge, but it's worth checking.
- **Postgame is often team-provided at a restaurant or the hotel.**

The player's control on a road trip is mostly through what they pack and what they choose from what's offered. The coach helps them do both well.

## The first-time-away-from-home transition

For many junior players, this is the first time they're responsible for feeding themselves. Some have never made breakfast before. Some have never made lunch. Some have never cooked dinner. Some have never made any of it. Their food knowledge is whatever they happened to pick up at home, which can range from "can make a few real meals" to "literally never operated a stove."

They might:

- Have never made meals of their own before, full stop.
- Default to whatever's easy — PB&J, cereal, frozen stuff, takeout.
- Skip meals because they don't know what to make.
- Eat the same three things on repeat because that's what they know.
- Be embarrassed to ask the billet for help.
- Not know what "real food" looks like on a budget or for an athlete.

This is normal. The coaching here is patient and foundational: get the basics working before optimizing anything. Three actual meals a day with real food beats a perfect pregame meal stacked on a foundation of toast.

## The "team chef" thing — and where it doesn't apply

Some pro teams have chefs. Some elite junior programs have meal services. **Most MJHL / WHL / OHL / QMJHL / USHL / BCHL teams do not.** The player is eating from billet food, team-provided road meals, restaurants, hotels, and whatever they make for themselves.

Don't coach as if there's a team meal plan. There usually isn't.

## Common food situations that catch new junior players

A short list of recurring traps:

- **PB&J before practice, starving by lunch.** Common breakfast pattern that under-fuels morning practices.
- **Pasta night every billet dinner.** The carbs are fine, but the same meal every night gets old and may not have enough protein or vegetables.
- **Skipping lunch on game days because of nerves.** Then under-fueled for the game.
- **Eating the team-provided postgame pizza and calling that recovery.** Better than nothing, but the protein and rehydration are missing.
- **Drinking energy drinks before games for the buzz.** Not nutrition. Affects sleep on the back end. A coaching conversation.
- **Bus snacks are chips, candy, gummies.** Default option from gas stations and team supplies. Manageable in moderation, not as the main fuel.
- **Cutting carbs because someone told them to lean out.** In-season, this almost always backfires for hockey players. See the physiology file on why.

## What the player will tell you about their life

When a player tells you about their week, listen for:

- **The billet dynamic** — who cooks, when, what.
- **The schedule pressure** — early-morning practices, late-night games, school in the mix.
- **The food landscape** — what's in the kitchen, what the player can make, what the team feeds them.
- **The road-trip patterns** — repeat opponents mean repeat food situations.
- **The social context** — what the team eats together, what's expected, what's "uncool" to ask for.

This is the world the coach has to operate in. Generic sports nutrition advice that ignores all of this doesn't help a junior hockey player. The coaching that does help is grounded in this reality.
